Investing Terminology

Understanding the basics is crucial to making sound investment decisions. Below are key terms that every investor should know.

1. Asset

What: Something valuable you own (resource), which can generate future economic benefits or increase in value over time.

Example: A house is an asset because its value might increase over time.

2. Capital Gain

What: The profit made when you sell something (like a stock or house) for more than you paid for it.

Example: If you buy stock for E50 and sell it for E70, you make E20 in capital gain.

3. Bond

What: A loan you give to a company or government that is repaid with interest.

Example: Buying a government bond means lending money to the government, and they pay you interest over time.

4. Index Fund

What: A fund that lets you invest in multiple companies by following a stock market index.

Example: Investing in an index fund means owning a small part of all the companies in that index.

5. Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF)

What: An ETF holds many investments (stocks or bonds) and is traded like a stock.

Example: ETFs allow you to invest in a variety of assets and trade them like regular stocks.

6. Mutual Fund

What: A collection of investments managed by professionals, funded by money from many investors.

Example: Investing in a mutual fund means your money is pooled with others to buy a range of investments.

7. Market Capitalization (Market Cap)

What: The total value of a company’s shares, calculated by multiplying stock price by total shares.

Example: Companies like Apple have a market cap of over a trillion dollars, indicating large value.

8. Risk

What: The potential for losing your investment. In investing, risk often refers to the permanent loss of capital.

Example: Investing E1,000 and selling for E400 means you’ve lost E600.

9. Return on Investment (ROI)

What: A percentage measure of the profit earned from an investment compared to the initial amount invested.

Example: Investing E1,000 and earning E200 means a 20% ROI.

10. Compound Interest

What: Interest that accumulates on both the original investment and previously earned interest.

Example: With compound interest, money grows faster the longer it's invested.

11. Liquidity

What: How quickly an asset can be converted into cash.

Example: Stocks have high liquidity as they can be sold quickly, whereas houses take longer.

12. Bull Market

What: A market condition where prices of assets rise (> 20%) due to investor confidence.

Example: In a bull market, investors buy stocks expecting prices to rise.

13. Bear Market

What: A market condition where prices of assets fall (> 20%), often due to economic downturn.

Example: During a bear market, investors sell stocks to prevent further losses.

14. Dematerialisation

What: Converting physical certificates into electronic form, credited to your Depository account.

15. Initial Public Offering (IPO)

What: The first time a company sells its shares to the public.

Example: Nkonyeni Pre-Cast Limited had its IPO in 2023, selling shares to the public for the first time.

16. Dividend Yield

What: A company’s annual dividend as a percentage of its stock price.

Example: A stock priced at E50 that pays a E2 dividend has a yield of 4%.

17. Asset Allocation

What: Dividing your investments across various asset types to balance risk and return.

Example: Allocating 60% to stocks and 40% to bonds for balanced growth and risk reduction.

18. Hedge Fund

What: A high-risk investment aiming to outperform traditional investments.

Example: The Medallion Fund returned over 30% per year after fees.

19. Short Selling

What: Borrowing stock, selling it, and buying it back later at a lower price to make a profit.

Example: Selling stock for E100 and repurchasing it for E80 yields a E20 profit.

20. Inflation

What: A general rise in prices over time, reducing the purchasing power of money.

Example: At 5% inflation, an item that costs E100 today will cost E105 next year.
